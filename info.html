History of Chess

The Origins in India

The earliest precursors of chess originated in ancient India during the Gupta Empire. The game was known as chaturanga, which means "four divisions of the military" in Sanskrit. These four divisions were represented in the game: infantry, cavalry, elephants, and chariots.

The Game Spreads to Persia

From India, chess spread to Persia. The Persians renamed the game chatrang. Under the Sasanian Empire, chatrang became a popular pastime among the nobility. The rules were refined, the number of pieces and board size were standardized, and chess strategy grew in sophistication.

The Game Moves to Arabia and Europe

The Islamic Golden Age saw chess spread from Persia to the Arab world. The Moors introduced chess to Spain during the early 8th century, and from there it spread throughout Western Europe. Various European languages adopted versions of the Persian name, with "checkmate" coming from "shah mat" meaning "the king is helpless".

Chess Becomes a Game of Skill

In Europe, chess transitioned from a dice-based game of chance to a game of pure skill during the 15th century. Additional rules were created, moves were restricted, and the queen and bishop pieces gained their modern powers. The modern rules of chess were established in the late 15th and early 16th centuries.

Chess Gains Popularity

By the 19th century, chess had become widely popular among people of the middle and upper classes in Europe and the Americas. Official world chess championships began in 1886. The 20th century saw the rise of grandmasters, opening theories, and chess engines. Today, chess remains a massively popular game and competitive sport around the world. Advancements in AI have even enabled chess programs that can defeat human grandmasters.

How to Play Chess

Basics

Chess is played between two opponents on opposite sides of a board with 64 squares arranged in an 8x8 grid. Each player controls 16 pieces: 1 king, 1 queen, 2 rooks, 2 bishops, 2 knights, and 8 pawns. The objective is to checkmate the other king by placing it under an inescapable threat.

The Board

Each type of piece moves differently. The king can move one square in any direction. The queen can move any number of squares in any straight line horizontally, vertically, or diagonally. Rooks can move any number of squares horizontally or vertically. Bishops can move any number of squares diagonally. Knights move two squares horizontally then one square vertically, or two squares vertically then one square horizontally. Pawns can only move forward one square at a time, except for their first move where they can move two squares.

The Pieces

On each turn, a player moves one of their pieces to a different square following the movement rules of that piece. All pieces capture opponent pieces by moving to the square occupied by the other piece. The captured piece is then removed from the board. Checkmate occurs when the king is under immediate attack and cannot avoid capture. Stalemate is when a player cannot move their pieces. Either player can capture the other's pieces according to movement rules.

The Ending

Players take turns moving one of their pieces. A game can end in checkmate, stalemate, or forfeit. If there is a capture threat, the defending player must get out of check. Double check or triple check is when there are multiple simultaneous threats. Perpetual check leads to a draw. Knights, bishops, and pawns cannot move to their same color complex if they have already moved to a different color complex.

Order / Professional Etiquette

White always moves first. Pieces can only move clockwise from their current position. Playing white has an advantage, but a skilled chess player can win with either color. Players should keep track of pieces captured to determine the difference in points between the sides. More points means an advantage toward the endgame.

Winning Chess Strategies

Control the center early.

Place pawns and pieces in the center four squares of the board to gain an advantage. The center provides greater mobility and access to more of the board.

Use all your pieces.

Don't just rely on the queen and more powerful pieces. Knights, bishops, and pawns also have an important role to play. Coordinating all your pieces creates threats that are hard for the opponent to defend against.

Think before each move.

Evaluate all possible moves and consider your opponent's responses. Try to set up "if-then" sequences by imagining what your opponent may play and how you will counter it. Each move should have a purpose.

Protect your king.

Castle early to keep your king safe and connect your rooks. Be very careful about moving your king in the opening and middlegame. Don't leave it exposed or in open lines of attack.

Gain a material advantage.

Try to capture opponent pieces in a way that gives you a net point gain while keeping the balance of power on the board. A material advantage gives you more attacking and defensive possibilities. But avoid just trading pieces without improving your position.

Maintain the initiative.

Force your opponent to respond to threats by creating attacks and weakening their position. When you have the initiative, you control the flow of the game and have more attacking chances. Seize the opportunity to launch an assault when your opponent makes a mistake.

Anticipate opponent threats.

Look for opportunities where your opponent could capture one of your pieces or launch an attack. Then, develop moves that counter those possible threats, forcing your opponent to abandon them. Always be one step ahead.
