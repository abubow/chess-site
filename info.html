History of Chess

Chess is a two-player strategy board game played on a chessboard, a checkered gameboard with 64 squares arranged in an eight-by-eight grid. Chess is played by millions of people worldwide, both amateurs and professionals. Each player begins the game with 16 pieces: one king, one queen, two rooks, two knights, two bishops, and eight pawns. Each of the six piece types moves differently. Pieces are used to attack and capture the opponent's pieces, with the objective to 'checkmate' the opponent's king by placing it under an inescapable threat of capture. In addition to checkmate, the game can be won by the voluntary resignation of the opponent, which typically occurs when too much material is lost, or if checkmate appears unavoidable. A game may result in a draw in several ways. Chess is believed to have originated in India, before 600 AD. The earliest known predecessor of chess may be the Indian game chaturanga, which was developed around the 6th century AD. The game chaturanga is also the likely ancestor of the Eastern strategy games xiangqi, janggi and shogi. Chess reached Europe by the 9th century, due to the Umayyad conquest of Hispania. The pieces assumed their current powers in Spain in the late 15th century; the modern rules were standardized in the 19th century. During the 20th century, chess became a recognized sport with international governing bodies, and the game's global popularity helped fuel a surge in tournament chess. The first World Chess Championship, officially recognised by FIDE, took place in 1886 with Wilhelm Steinitz defeating Johannes Zukertort. Since 1948, the World Championship has been controlled by FIDE; the current World Champion is Magnus Carlsen. The highest official rating a chess player can achieve is 2850. There are many variants of chess. Three of the most popular are chess960, the correspondence game, and chess problems. Chess has been a part of the official program of the Summer Olympic Games since 1972. The World Chess Championship has been conducted since 1886, although the tournament format was not established until 1921. The game is popular in schools, where it can improve concentration, logic, and reasoning skills. Chess is also popular among the general public, with recreational players of all ages and abilities.
